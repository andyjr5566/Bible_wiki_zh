Joseph and Potiphar’s Wife

Genesis 39:1

Meanwhile, Joseph had been taken down to Egypt

Joseph's journey to Egypt marks a significant turning point in his life and in the narrative of Genesis. This phrase indicates a transition from his life in Canaan to a new chapter in Egypt. The term "down" reflects the geographical descent from the highlands of Canaan to the lower elevation of Egypt. This movement also symbolizes a descent into trials and tribulations, which God uses to fulfill His greater plan. Joseph's journey to Egypt foreshadows the later descent of the Israelites into Egypt, leading to their eventual enslavement and the Exodus.
where an Egyptian named Potiphar

Potiphar is introduced as a key figure in Joseph's life in Egypt. His name, likely of Egyptian origin, suggests a person of significant status and influence. Potiphar's role in the narrative sets the stage for Joseph's rise and fall within his household, which ultimately leads to Joseph's imprisonment and subsequent rise to power. Potiphar's position in Egyptian society provides insight into the hierarchical and bureaucratic nature of ancient Egypt.
an officer of Pharaoh and captain of the guard

Potiphar's titles indicate his high-ranking position in Pharaoh's court. As an officer and captain of the guard, Potiphar would have been responsible for the security of the Pharaoh and possibly involved in military or police functions. This highlights the level of trust and authority he held, making his household a place of significant influence. Joseph's placement in Potiphar's house is a strategic move in God's plan, positioning him close to the centers of power in Egypt.
bought him from the Ishmaelites

The mention of the Ishmaelites connects Joseph's story to the broader narrative of Abraham's descendants. The Ishmaelites, descendants of Ishmael, Abraham's son by Hagar, were known as traders and caravan leaders. This transaction underscores the practice of slavery in the ancient Near East and the economic activities that connected different regions. Joseph's sale into slavery is a pivotal moment that sets the stage for his eventual rise to power in Egypt.
who had taken him there

The Ishmaelites' role in transporting Joseph to Egypt highlights the movement and trade routes of the ancient world. This phrase emphasizes the providential nature of Joseph's journey, as God uses the actions of others to bring about His purposes. Joseph's arrival in Egypt is not merely a result of human actions but part of a divine plan that will lead to the preservation of his family and the fulfillment of God's promises to Abraham.

Genesis 39:2

And the LORD was with Joseph

This phrase emphasizes the divine presence and favor upon Joseph, a recurring theme in his life. The LORD's presence signifies not only protection but also guidance and blessing. This echoes the covenantal promise God made to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, ensuring His faithfulness to their descendants. The presence of God with Joseph is a precursor to the New Testament promise of God being with believers through the Holy Spirit (Matthew 28:20).
and he became a successful man

Joseph's success is attributed directly to the LORD's presence, highlighting the biblical principle that true prosperity comes from God. Despite being in a foreign land and initially a slave, Joseph's rise to success demonstrates God's ability to bless His people in any circumstance. This success is not merely material but also includes wisdom and favor, reminiscent of Solomon's request for wisdom and subsequent prosperity (1 Kings 3:10-14).
serving in the household of his Egyptian master

Joseph's role as a servant in Potiphar's house places him in a position of humility and obedience, yet it is here that his leadership and administrative skills are honed. This setting in Egypt, a powerful and advanced civilization, provides a backdrop for Joseph's eventual rise to power. The cultural context of servitude in Egypt contrasts with Joseph's later authority, illustrating a theme of reversal and redemption. Joseph's service foreshadows Christ's own servanthood, as He came not to be served but to serve (Mark 10:45).

Joseph's time in prison is a period of testing and preparation. Despite the injustice he faced, Joseph remained faithful to God, which is a recurring theme in his life. This period of suffering and waiting is reminiscent of other biblical figures who endured hardship before being elevated by God, such as David and Daniel. Joseph's experience in prison also serves as a type of Christ, who was unjustly accused and suffered before being exalted. The narrative emphasizes God's sovereignty and the idea that He can use even the most difficult circumstances for His purposes.

Genesis 39:21

the LORD was with him

This phrase emphasizes God's continual presence with Joseph, even in dire circumstances. Throughout Joseph's life, God's presence is a recurring theme, seen earlier in Genesis 39:2 when Joseph was successful in Potiphar's house. This reflects the covenantal promise God made to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, ensuring His presence with their descendants. It also prefigures the New Testament promise of God's presence with believers, as seen in Matthew 28:20, where Jesus assures His followers, "I am with you always."
and extended kindness to him

The Hebrew word for "kindness" here is "chesed," often translated as loving-kindness or steadfast love. This term is frequently used in the Old Testament to describe God's covenantal love and mercy. In Joseph's context, it signifies God's active role in providing for and protecting him, even in prison. This kindness is a precursor to the grace extended through Jesus Christ, who embodies God's ultimate act of kindness and mercy.
granting him favor in the eyes of the prison warden

Joseph's favor with the prison warden mirrors his earlier favor with Potiphar, indicating a divine orchestration of events. This favor is reminiscent of Daniel's experience in Babylon, where God granted him favor with the authorities (Daniel 1:9). It highlights a biblical principle that God can influence the hearts of leaders and authorities, as seen in Proverbs 21:1, which states, "The king’s heart is a stream of water in the hand of the LORD; He turns it wherever He will." This favor also foreshadows the favor Jesus found with God and man, as mentioned in Luke 2:52.

Genesis 39:22

And the warden put all the prisoners under Joseph’s care

Joseph's rise to a position of authority within the prison reflects a recurring theme in his life: God's favor and providence. Despite being unjustly imprisoned, Joseph's integrity and leadership qualities are recognized by the warden. This mirrors his earlier experience in Potiphar's house, where he was also entrusted with significant responsibility. The warden's decision to place all prisoners under Joseph's care indicates a high level of trust and suggests that Joseph's character and abilities were evident to those around him. This situation foreshadows Joseph's future role in Egypt, where he will again be placed in a position of great authority.
so that he was responsible for all that was done in the prison

Joseph's responsibility for all activities within the prison highlights his administrative skills and reliability. This phrase underscores the complete trust the warden had in Joseph, allowing him to manage the daily operations and welfare of the prisoners. It also reflects a broader biblical principle of stewardship, where those who are faithful in small things are often entrusted with greater responsibilities (Luke 16:10). Joseph's management of the prison can be seen as a type of Christ, who also took on the burdens of others and served with humility and faithfulness. This passage illustrates how God can use adverse circumstances to prepare His people for future service and leadership.

Genesis 39:23

The warden did not concern himself with anything under Joseph’s care

This phrase highlights Joseph's exceptional trustworthiness and competence. In the context of ancient Egyptian prisons, a warden would typically oversee all activities and ensure order. However, Joseph's integrity and God's favor allowed him to gain the warden's complete trust. This mirrors Joseph's earlier experience in Potiphar's house, where he was also entrusted with significant responsibility. The trust placed in Joseph foreshadows his future role as a leader in Egypt, demonstrating how God prepares His chosen servants for greater tasks.
because the LORD was with Joseph

This phrase emphasizes the divine presence and favor that Joseph experienced throughout his life. The recurring theme of God's presence with Joseph is a testament to the covenant relationship between God and His people. It reflects the broader biblical narrative where God's presence signifies blessing, guidance, and protection. This divine companionship is a precursor to the New Testament promise of God's presence with believers through the Holy Spirit. Joseph's life serves as a type of Christ, who also experienced God's presence and favor in His earthly ministry.
and gave him success in whatever he did

The success Joseph experienced is attributed directly to God's intervention. This success is not merely material or professional but encompasses all aspects of his life, reflecting the biblical principle that true prosperity comes from God. Joseph's ability to thrive in adverse circumstances is a testament to God's sovereignty and faithfulness. This phrase connects to other scriptural promises of prosperity for those who are faithful to God, such as in Psalm 1:3 and Joshua 1:8. Joseph's life foreshadows the ultimate success and victory found in Jesus Christ, who perfectly fulfilled God's will.
