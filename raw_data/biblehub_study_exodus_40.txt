Setting Up the Tabernacle

Exodus 40:1

Then

This word indicates a continuation of the narrative, suggesting a sequence of events. It connects to the preceding chapters where the instructions for the construction of the Tabernacle were given. The timing is significant as it follows the completion of the Tabernacle's construction, marking a transition from instruction to action.
the LORD

This refers to Yahweh, the covenant name of God revealed to Moses at the burning bush (Exodus 3:14). It emphasizes God's personal relationship with Israel and His role as their divine leader. The use of this name underscores the authority and holiness of the instructions that follow.
said

This indicates direct communication from God, highlighting the prophetic nature of Moses' leadership. It reflects the unique relationship Moses had with God, where he received divine revelation directly, as seen throughout the Pentateuch.
to Moses

Moses is the central human figure in the Exodus narrative, serving as the mediator between God and the Israelites. His role as a prophet and leader is crucial, as he is responsible for conveying God's commands to the people. This phrase underscores Moses' obedience and faithfulness, which are key themes in his life and ministry. Moses' leadership is a type of Christ, who is the ultimate mediator between God and humanity (1 Timothy 2:5).

Exodus 40:2

On the first day of the first month

This phrase indicates a specific time for the setting up of the tabernacle, aligning with the beginning of the Hebrew religious calendar, which is Nisan (March-April). This timing is significant as it marks the anniversary of the Exodus from Egypt, symbolizing a new beginning for the Israelites. The first month is also when Passover is celebrated, linking the tabernacle's establishment to themes of deliverance and redemption.
you are to set up the tabernacle

The tabernacle, also known as the "Tent of Meeting," was a portable sanctuary that served as the dwelling place of God among the Israelites during their wilderness journey. Its construction was commanded by God and detailed in earlier chapters of Exodus. The tabernacle's setup signifies God's presence and guidance, emphasizing the importance of worship and obedience. The tabernacle prefigures the coming of Jesus Christ, who "tabernacled" among us (John 1:14), representing God's presence in a more permanent and personal way.
the Tent of Meeting

This term highlights the tabernacle's function as a place where God would meet with Moses and the Israelites. It was the central place of worship and sacrifice, where the covenant relationship between God and His people was maintained. The Tent of Meeting served as a precursor to the temple in Jerusalem and ultimately points to Jesus as the ultimate mediator between God and humanity (1 Timothy 2:5). The tabernacle's design and rituals foreshadow the work of Christ, who fulfills the law and the prophets.

Exodus 40:3

Put the ark of the Testimony in it

The "ark of the Testimony" refers to the Ark of the Covenant, a sacred chest that held the tablets of the Ten Commandments, which were the core of God's covenant with Israel. The Ark symbolized God's presence and His covenant with His people. It was constructed according to specific instructions given by God to Moses on Mount Sinai (Exodus 25:10-22). The Ark was made of acacia wood and overlaid with gold, signifying its holiness and the divine presence. The term "Testimony" underscores the Ark's role as a witness to the covenant between God and Israel. Placing the Ark in the Tabernacle's Most Holy Place (Holy of Holies) was crucial, as it was the focal point of God's dwelling among His people. The Ark prefigures Christ, who is the ultimate revelation of God's presence and the fulfillment of the Law.
and screen off the ark with the veil

The "veil" was a curtain that separated the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place within the Tabernacle. This veil was made of fine linen and blue, purple, and scarlet yarn, with cherubim skillfully woven into it (Exodus 26:31-33). The veil served as a barrier, signifying the separation between a holy God and sinful humanity. Only the high priest could pass beyond the veil, and only once a year on the Day of Atonement, to make atonement for the sins of the people (Leviticus 16). This separation highlights the holiness of God and the need for atonement. In the New Testament, the tearing of the temple veil at the moment of Christ's death (Matthew 27:51) symbolizes the removal of the barrier between God and humanity, granting believers direct access to God through Jesus Christ, our High Priest.

Exodus 40:4

Then bring in the table

The table referred to here is the Table of Showbread, which was placed in the Holy Place of the Tabernacle. This table was made of acacia wood and overlaid with pure gold, symbolizing purity and divinity. The table held the Bread of the Presence, which represented God's provision and fellowship with His people. In a broader biblical context, the table signifies God's sustenance and the covenant relationship between God and Israel. The table's placement in the Tabernacle underscores the importance of worship and communion with God.
and set out its arrangement;

The arrangement refers to the specific placement of the twelve loaves of bread on the table, which were to be set in two rows of six. This arrangement was not arbitrary but followed divine instructions, symbolizing the twelve tribes of Israel and God's continual provision for them. The bread was replaced every Sabbath, and the old bread was consumed by the priests, indicating a perpetual covenant and the sustenance provided by God. This practice foreshadows the spiritual nourishment provided by Christ, who is the "bread of life" (John 6:35).
bring in the lampstand as well,

The lampstand, or menorah, was also made of pure gold and was placed opposite the table in the Holy Place. It had seven branches and was designed to provide light within the Tabernacle. The lampstand symbolizes the light of God's presence and guidance. In the New Testament, Jesus refers to Himself as the "light of the world" (John 8:12), drawing a parallel to the lampstand's function in the Tabernacle. The continual burning of the lamps signifies the perpetual presence and illumination of God among His people.
and set up its lamps.

Setting up the lamps involved ensuring they were filled with pure olive oil and trimmed to burn brightly. This task was the responsibility of the priests and was to be done regularly, emphasizing the need for constant vigilance and care in maintaining the light. The lamps' light represents the guidance and revelation of God, illuminating the path for His people. This imagery is echoed in Psalm 119:105, where God's word is described as a "lamp to my feet and a light to my path." The maintenance of the lamps also points to the believer's responsibility to remain spiritually vigilant and filled with the Holy Spirit, who empowers and enlightens.

Exodus 40:5

Place the gold altar of incense

The gold altar of incense was a small altar located in the Holy Place of the tabernacle, symbolizing the prayers of the people ascending to God. The use of gold signifies purity and divinity, reflecting the holiness required in worship. Incense was a common element in ancient Near Eastern religious practices, but in Israel, it was specifically prescribed by God, emphasizing the distinctiveness of Israelite worship. The altar's placement and use foreshadow Christ's role as our intercessor, as seen in Hebrews 7:25, where Jesus continually intercedes for believers.
in front of the ark of the Testimony

The ark of the Testimony, also known as the Ark of the Covenant, was the most sacred object in the tabernacle, containing the tablets of the Law. Its presence signified God's covenant with Israel and His dwelling among His people. The altar of incense being placed in front of the ark highlights the importance of prayer and intercession in maintaining the covenant relationship. This positioning also points to the veil that separated the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place, which was torn at Christ's crucifixion (Matthew 27:51), symbolizing direct access to God through Jesus.
and hang the curtain at the entrance to the tabernacle

The curtain, or veil, at the entrance of the tabernacle served as a barrier between the sacred space and the outside world, emphasizing the holiness of God and the need for separation from sin. This curtain was part of a series of veils that marked increasing levels of sanctity within the tabernacle structure. The tabernacle itself was a portable sanctuary, reflecting the nomadic lifestyle of the Israelites during their wilderness journey. The curtain's function and symbolism are fulfilled in Christ, who is described as the "new and living way" through the veil (Hebrews 10:20), granting believers access to the presence of God.

Exodus 40:6

Place the altar of burnt offering

The altar of burnt offering, also known as the brazen altar, was central to the sacrificial system established by God for the Israelites. It was made of acacia wood and overlaid with bronze, symbolizing judgment and sacrifice. This altar was where offerings were made to atone for sin, pointing to the ultimate sacrifice of Jesus Christ, who fulfilled the sacrificial system through His death on the cross (Hebrews 10:10). The altar's placement signifies the necessity of atonement before entering into God's presence.
in front of the entrance

Positioning the altar in front of the entrance underscores the idea that one must first deal with sin before approaching God. This reflects the broader biblical principle that reconciliation with God requires a mediator and a sacrifice, as seen in the role of Jesus as the mediator of the new covenant (1 Timothy 2:5). The altar's location also served as a constant reminder to the Israelites of the need for purification and repentance.
to the tabernacle,

The tabernacle, also known as the Tent of Meeting, was the portable dwelling place of God among the Israelites during their journey through the wilderness. It was a sacred space where God met with His people, symbolizing His presence and guidance. The tabernacle's design and function foreshadowed the coming of Christ, who "tabernacled" among us (John 1:14), providing a way for humanity to dwell with God.
the Tent of Meeting.

The Tent of Meeting was a term used interchangeably with the tabernacle, emphasizing its role as a place of divine encounter. It was here that Moses spoke with God "face to face" (Exodus 33:11), highlighting the intimate relationship between God and His chosen leader. This concept of meeting with God is fulfilled in the New Testament through Jesus, who provides believers with direct access to the Father (Hebrews 4:16). The Tent of Meeting served as a precursor to the church, the body of Christ, where believers gather to worship and commune with God.

Exodus 40:7

And place the basin

The basin, also known as the laver, was a large bronze vessel used for ceremonial washing. It symbolizes purification and the need for cleanliness before approaching God. The use of bronze, a durable and reflective material, may signify the enduring nature of God's requirements for holiness. The basin's placement and use highlight the importance of ritual purity in worship practices.
between the Tent of Meeting and the altar

The Tent of Meeting, or Tabernacle, was the portable earthly dwelling place of God among the Israelites. The altar, specifically the bronze altar, was used for burnt offerings. Placing the basin between these two significant structures underscores its role as a mediator between sacrifice and divine presence. This positioning reflects the necessity of cleansing before entering into God's presence or offering sacrifices, a theme echoed in Psalm 24:3-4, which speaks of having clean hands and a pure heart.
and put water in it

Water in the basin was used by the priests for washing their hands and feet before performing their duties, as commanded in Exodus 30:18-21. This act of washing symbolizes spiritual cleansing and preparation for service. Water is a recurring symbol of purification throughout Scripture, seen in the New Testament with the concept of baptism (e.g., John 3:5, Ephesians 5:26). The emphasis on water here prefigures the cleansing work of Jesus Christ, who offers living water and purification from sin (John 4:10, 1 John 1:7).

Exodus 40:8

Set up the surrounding courtyard

The courtyard was an essential part of the Tabernacle, serving as a boundary that separated the sacred space from the common areas. It was a place where the Israelites could gather and offer sacrifices, symbolizing a transition from the secular to the holy. The courtyard measured 100 cubits long and 50 cubits wide, as described in Exodus 27:9-19. This space was enclosed by linen curtains, which were supported by bronze pillars. The use of bronze, a durable metal, signifies strength and endurance, reflecting the enduring nature of God's covenant with Israel. The courtyard's design emphasized the holiness of the Tabernacle, reminding the Israelites of the need for purity and reverence when approaching God.
and hang the curtain for the entrance to the courtyard.

The entrance curtain, also known as the screen, was made of finely twisted linen and blue, purple, and scarlet yarn, as detailed in Exodus 27:16. This curtain served as the sole entry point into the courtyard, symbolizing the exclusivity and sanctity of access to God's presence. The colors used in the curtain were significant, with blue representing divinity, purple symbolizing royalty, and scarlet indicating sacrifice. These colors foreshadowed the coming of Jesus Christ, who is the divine King and ultimate sacrifice. The curtain's function as a barrier also pointed to the separation between God and humanity due to sin, a separation that would later be bridged by Christ's atoning work. The act of hanging the curtain was a final step in preparing the Tabernacle for worship, underscoring the importance of order and obedience in the worship of God.

Exodus 40:9

Take the anointing oil

The anointing oil was a sacred mixture described in Exodus 30:22-25, composed of myrrh, cinnamon, calamus, cassia, and olive oil. This oil was used exclusively for consecrating the tabernacle and its contents, symbolizing the Holy Spirit's presence and sanctification. The use of oil in anointing signifies setting apart for God's purposes, a practice seen throughout Scripture, including the anointing of kings and priests.
and anoint the tabernacle and everything in it;

The tabernacle was the portable dwelling place for the presence of God among the Israelites during their journey through the wilderness. Anointing the tabernacle and its contents signified dedicating them to God's service. This act of anointing foreshadows the New Testament understanding of believers as temples of the Holy Spirit (1 Corinthians 6:19), set apart for God's purposes.
consecrate it along with all its furnishings,

To consecrate means to make or declare something sacred, dedicating it to divine service. The furnishings of the tabernacle, including the altar, lampstand, and table of showbread, were integral to the worship and sacrificial system established by God. This consecration underscores the holiness required in approaching God, a theme echoed in Hebrews 9:21-22, which speaks of the necessity of purification through blood.
and it shall be holy.

Holiness is a central theme in the Bible, reflecting God's nature and His requirement for His people. The tabernacle's holiness was not inherent but derived from God's presence and the consecration process. This mirrors the call for believers to be holy as God is holy (1 Peter 1:15-16), emphasizing a life set apart for God's glory. The tabernacle's holiness also points to Jesus Christ, who fulfilled the law and the prophets, becoming the ultimate holy temple through His life, death, and resurrection.

Exodus 40:10

Anoint the altar of burnt offering and all its utensils;

The anointing of the altar signifies setting it apart for sacred use, a practice rooted in the ancient Near Eastern tradition of consecrating objects for divine service. The altar of burnt offering, located in the Tabernacle's outer court, was central to Israelite worship, symbolizing atonement and reconciliation with God. Anointing with oil, often a mixture of olive oil and spices, represents the Holy Spirit's presence and empowerment. This act parallels the anointing of priests and kings, indicating the altar's vital role in mediating between God and His people. The utensils, including basins, forks, and firepans, were also anointed, emphasizing the holiness required in every aspect of worship.
consecrate the altar,

Consecration involves dedicating something entirely to God, making it holy and set apart from common use. The altar's consecration underscores its role as a place where sacrifices are offered, symbolizing the people's devotion and obedience to God. This act of consecration is a physical manifestation of spiritual purity and commitment, reflecting the broader covenant relationship between God and Israel. The altar's holiness is a reminder of God's presence and the need for purity in approaching Him, foreshadowing the ultimate consecration found in Jesus Christ, who fulfills the sacrificial system through His atoning death.
and it shall be most holy.

The designation "most holy" elevates the altar's status, indicating its supreme sanctity within the Tabernacle. This term is used for the most sacred objects and spaces, such as the Holy of Holies, where God's presence dwelt. The altar's holiness signifies its critical function in the sacrificial system, serving as the focal point for offerings that maintain the covenant relationship. This concept of holiness is central to understanding God's nature and His requirements for His people. In the New Testament, Jesus embodies this holiness, becoming the ultimate sacrifice and high priest, providing believers with direct access to God.

Exodus 40:11

Anoint the basin and its stand

The act of anointing in the Old Testament often signifies setting apart something or someone for a holy purpose. The basin, also known as the laver, was used by the priests for ceremonial washing before they entered the Tent of Meeting or approached the altar. This ritual cleansing symbolized purification and readiness to serve God. The stand, which held the basin, was also anointed, indicating that every part of the tabernacle's furnishings was to be dedicated to God. This practice underscores the importance of purity and holiness in worship, as seen in other scriptures like Leviticus 8:10-11, where Moses anoints the tabernacle and all its furnishings.
and consecrate them

To consecrate means to declare or set apart as sacred. In the context of the tabernacle, consecration involved a ceremonial act that made the objects fit for divine service. This process was not merely symbolic but was a divine requirement for the Israelites, emphasizing the holiness required in approaching God. The consecration of the basin and its stand highlights the necessity of sanctification in worship, a theme echoed in the New Testament, where believers are called to be holy (1 Peter 1:15-16). The consecration of these items can also be seen as a type of Christ, who is the ultimate means of purification and sanctification for believers, as described in Hebrews 10:10.

Exodus 40:12

Then bring Aaron and his sons

This phrase indicates the selection of Aaron and his sons for a special role within the Israelite community. Aaron, the brother of Moses, was chosen by God to be the first high priest, and his sons were to serve as priests. This establishes the Aaronic priesthood, which is a hereditary priesthood passed down through Aaron's lineage. The priesthood is a type of Christ, who is our High Priest according to the order of Melchizedek, as described in Hebrews 7. The selection of Aaron and his sons underscores the importance of divine calling and ordination in spiritual leadership.
to the entrance to the Tent of Meeting

The Tent of Meeting, also known as the Tabernacle, was the portable earthly dwelling place of God among the Israelites during their journey through the wilderness. It was a sacred space where God would meet with Moses and the priests. The entrance to the Tent of Meeting was a significant location, symbolizing the threshold between the common and the holy. This setting emphasizes the need for purification and preparation before entering into God's presence, reflecting the holiness required to approach God.
and wash them with water

The act of washing with water signifies purification and consecration. In the ancient Near Eastern context, washing was a common ritual for cleansing and preparing individuals for religious duties. This washing symbolizes the need for spiritual cleansing and sanctification before serving in the presence of God. It prefigures the New Testament concept of baptism, which represents the washing away of sin and the believer's identification with Christ's death, burial, and resurrection. The washing of Aaron and his sons highlights the necessity of purity and holiness in those who minister before the Lord.

Exodus 40:13

And you are to clothe Aaron with the holy garments

The clothing of Aaron with holy garments signifies the setting apart of the priesthood. These garments were specifically designed by divine instruction (Exodus 28) and symbolized purity, holiness, and the glory of God. The garments included the ephod, breastplate, robe, tunic, turban, and sash, each with its own symbolic meaning. The act of clothing Aaron represents the covering of sin and the righteousness required to approach God, prefiguring the righteousness of Christ that covers believers (Isaiah 61:10).
anoint him

Anointing with oil was a common practice in the ancient Near East for consecrating individuals to a sacred office. The oil, often a mixture of olive oil and spices, symbolized the Holy Spirit's presence and empowerment. In Aaron's case, it marked his divine appointment and the beginning of his priestly duties. This anointing foreshadows the anointing of Jesus as the Messiah (meaning "Anointed One") and the anointing of believers with the Holy Spirit (1 John 2:20, 27).
and consecrate him

Consecration involves setting someone or something apart for a sacred purpose. For Aaron, this meant being dedicated entirely to the service of God. The process included sacrifices and rituals that underscored the seriousness of approaching a holy God. This consecration points to the ultimate consecration of Jesus, who was set apart to fulfill the role of the perfect High Priest (Hebrews 7:26-28).
so that he may serve Me as a priest

The role of the priest was to mediate between God and the people, offering sacrifices and prayers on their behalf. Aaron's service as a priest was foundational for the Levitical priesthood, which continued until the time of Christ. This priestly service is a type of Christ's eternal priesthood, as He serves as the ultimate mediator between God and humanity (1 Timothy 2:5, Hebrews 9:11-12). The priesthood of Aaron also prefigures the priesthood of all believers, who are called to offer spiritual sacrifices to God (1 Peter 2:5, 9).

It was meticulously designed with specific materials and dimensions, reflecting the holiness and order of God. The setting up of the tabernacle marked the completion of a significant phase in Israel's journey, as it provided a tangible place for worship and sacrifice.
on the first day of the first month

This timing is significant as it aligns with the beginning of the religious calendar for the Israelites, which was established at the time of the Exodus from Egypt (Exodus 12:2). The first month, known as Nisan or Abib, was a time of renewal and new beginnings, symbolizing liberation and the start of a new life for the Israelites. This phrase marks the culmination of the instructions given to Moses on Mount Sinai regarding the construction of the tabernacle (Exodus 25-31). The tabernacle, also known as the "Tent of Meeting," served as the dwelling place of God among the Israelites during their wilderness journey. The act of setting up the tabernacle signifies obedience to God's detailed instructions and the beginning of a new phase in Israel's relationship with God.
